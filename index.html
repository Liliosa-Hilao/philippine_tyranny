Liliosa Hilao: A Martyr of Martial Law

Liliosa Rapi Hilao (March 14, 1950 – April 5, 1973) was a Filipina student journalist and activist who became a symbol of resistance against the oppressive rule of Martial Law in the Philippines. She is remembered as the first political detainee to die in detention under Martial Law.

Early Life and Activism

Liliosa Hilao was born on March 14, 1950, in Bulan, Sorsogon. The seventh of nine children, she had seven sisters and one brother. From an early age, Liliosa demonstrated exceptional academic talent, consistently earning honors throughout her elementary and high school years. She pursued her passion for communication at the Pamantasan ng Lungsod ng Maynila (PLM), where she majored in Communication Arts. Liliosa's involvement in student life was extensive and impactful. She served as an editor for the university's student paper, *Hasik*, honing her journalistic skills and using her voice to address critical issues. Her leadership qualities were evident in her role as the student president of the communication arts department. She also represented her fellow students in PLM's student central government and served as secretary of the Women's Club of Pamantasan. Her commitment to student advocacy extended to her membership in the College Editors Guild of the Philippines. Liliosa also founded the Communication Arts Club at PLM, further demonstrating her initiative and dedication to her field. While she was known to be sickly and did not participate in street protests, she channeled her activism through her powerful writing. Her articles, including "The Vietnamization of the Philippines" and "Democracy is Dead in the Philippines under Martial Law," eloquently expressed her opposition to the Marcos regime and its policies.

“Democracy is dead.” - Liliosa Hilao, in her diary, upon the declaration of Martial Law.

Arrest and Death

The events leading to Liliosa's tragic death began on April 4, 1973, when the Philippine Constabulary Anti-Narcotics Unit (CANU) raided the Hilao family residence. Despite not presenting a search warrant or arrest order, the CANU soldiers, led by Lt. Arturo Castillo, forcibly entered the house. Liliosa was not present during the initial raid but arrived later that evening. Upon her arrival, she was immediately subjected to brutal treatment by Lt. Castillo, who repeatedly beat her. Family members who attempted to intervene were threatened by the soldiers. The following morning, April 5th, Liliosa was handcuffed and taken to the CANU office at Camp Crame for questioning. Her sister, Josefina, was also arrested and taken to Camp Crame. While Josefina was not allowed to speak with Liliosa, she witnessed her sister's face, which appeared beaten and bloodied, a clear indication of the torture and abuse she had endured. Three days later, on April 7th, the Hilao family received a phone call informing them that Liliosa was in critical condition at the Camp Crame Station Hospital. When her sister, Alice, arrived at the hospital, she found Liliosa in the emergency room, her face disfigured and her body bearing visible marks of severe torture, including needle puncture marks and an "opening at her throat." The room where Liliosa was held had a strong smell of formalin. Despite her critical condition, no medical staff attended to her. Shortly after Alice’s arrival, she and Josefina were taken to the CANU Office where they were informed by Lt. Castillo that Liliosa had died. The official necropsy report listed cardio-respiratory arrest as the cause of death, a finding that was widely disputed by her family and friends. The family strongly suspected that the real cause of her death was the torture she suffered at the hands of the military. The Philippine Constabulary offered the family 2,200 pesos for burial expenses, an offer they refused to accept.

Aftermath and Legacy

Liliosa Hilao's death sent shockwaves through the community and became a powerful symbol of the brutality of Martial Law. Her case became a rallying cry for justice and human rights. Following the People Power Revolution in 1986, Liliosa's family filed a lawsuit against former President Ferdinand Marcos, alleging torture, rape, and the use of muriatic acid in her death. The lawsuit, filed in US courts under the Alien Tort Statute, marked a significant legal challenge against Marcos and his regime. In 1996, the Court of Appeals in Southern California ruled in favor of the Hilao family and other human rights victims, awarding them nearly $2 billion in damages. However, the Marcos family’s efforts to conceal their assets have made it difficult to fully enforce the judgment. Despite the challenges in obtaining full compensation, the legal victory was a symbolic triumph for the victims of Martial Law. Liliosa Hilao's legacy extends far beyond the courtroom. She is remembered as a courageous student leader, a talented writer, and a symbol of resistance against tyranny. Her name is inscribed on the Bantayog ng mga Bayani's Wall of Remembrance, honoring the heroes and martyrs of Martial Law. In her hometown of Bulan, Sorsogon, a street bears her name, ensuring that her memory and her sacrifice will not be forgotten. Liliosa Hilao’s story serves as a potent reminder of the cost of oppression and the importance of defending human rights and democratic freedoms. She continues to inspire generations of Filipinos to fight for justice and to uphold the principles of democracy and human dignity.
